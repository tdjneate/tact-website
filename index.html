Reimagining communication technology with the people who need it most

TACT is a UKRI Future Leaders Fellowship co-designing new communication technologies with people with aphasia and children with developmental language disorders.

The Gap

Communication technology wasn't designed for everyone

Millions of people rely on gestures, expressions, drawing, and fragments of speech to communicate. Yet assistive technologies remain rigid, language-heavy, and built around assumptions of "normal" communication.

20% of us will experience communication disabilities

Royal College of SLTs, 2022

1 in 3 stroke survivors develop aphasia

Stroke Association, 2016

3 children per classroom struggle with speech and language

Technology should support total communication — the full range of strategies people use to make meaning, not just the ones that fit neatly into a screen.

Our Communities

Designing with, not for

TACT works with two communities whose communication needs are underserved by current technologies. They are co-designers, not just participants.

People with Aphasia

Aphasia is an acquired communication disability, most often following stroke. It affects the ability to speak, understand, read, and write — but not intelligence. People with aphasia use rich combinations of gesture, drawing, expression, and speech to communicate. We partner with Aphasia Reconnect and Dyscover to co-design technologies that support these strategies rather than replacing them.

Children & Young People

1.9 million children in the UK struggle with talking and understanding words. For those with developmental language disorders, navigating school, friendships, and daily life can feel isolating. Through Speech and Language UK, we work with secondary school children aged 11–18 as co-designers — bringing their perspectives into the design of technologies that fit their lives, not just clinical settings.

Our Approach

Four years. Four stages.

TACT is a 48-month research programme that moves from understanding communication to building, testing, and releasing open-source tools.

1

Understand

Ethnographic fieldwork and interviews with people with aphasia, children with DLD, and speech-language therapists — mapping communication strategies, challenges, and the real-world contexts that matter most.

Months 1–8 · Ethnography · Interviews · Go-Along Research
2

Co-Design

Envisioning and building communication technologies with our communities — using accessible, innovative co-design methods that work when language itself is a barrier.

Months 7–29 · Participatory Design · Prototyping · Wearables · AI
3

Evaluate

Testing our technologies where they'll actually be used — on public transport, in cafés, at Science Gallery London, and in remote settings like video calls.

Months 18–37 · Lab Studies · In-Situ Evaluation · Real-World Settings
4

Release

An open-source toolkit of communication technologies — free apps, design resources, and training materials for people with communication challenges, families, therapists, and developers.

Months 37–48 · Open-Source Toolkit · Training · Public Launch
